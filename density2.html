Conspiracy theories are living their Golden Age (politico.eu): fostered by online platforms, social media and uncertain times, they have been spreading among people in different ways and through various channels. Some of the most recent theories have started to spread from user-generated content published online, to the extent that hashtags like #saveourchildren where used to share QAnon’s messages on Instagram and YouTube’s algorithm suggested videos about ‘Flat Earth’ theories. These platforms have been trying to counter this issue by censoring conspiracy-related content, but these theories seem not to stop from circulating online. The research revolves around the aesthetic side of conspiracy theories by analyzing their form and visual language across different channels and platforms. The investigated sub-topics are: the look and feel of conspiracy-related posts on Instagram, the appearance of conspiracies’ gadgets on Etsy, the inspiration behind Redbubble’s conspiracy-themed stickers and the usage of memes’ characters on 4chan posts. The goal of the research is to show how the bottom-up nature of conspiracy theories affects the language in which they are expressed, which is often inspired by grassroots means of communication used by common Internet users.
